About Chinese Spring Festival

Chinese Spring Festival, also known as Chinese New Year or Lunar New Year, is the most important and widely celebrated traditional festival in China. It marks the beginning of the lunar new year and is a time for family reunions, feasting, and various cultural activities. The festival typically lasts for 15 days, from the eve of the lunar new year to the Lantern Festival.

This centuries-old celebration is steeped in tradition and symbolism, with each custom designed to bring good fortune, happiness, and prosperity for the coming year. From the iconic red decorations to the spectacular dragon dances, Chinese Spring Festival is a vibrant display of Chinese culture and heritage.

History & Origins

The origins of Chinese Spring Festival date back more than 3,000 years to the Shang Dynasty (1600-1046 BCE). According to legend, there was a mythical beast called "Nian" that would come out on the eve of the new year to eat villagers and destroy their homes. People discovered that Nian was afraid of loud noises, bright lights, and the color red.

To protect themselves, villagers would hang red lanterns and scrolls on their windows and doors, set off firecrackers, and stay up late with bright lights. These practices evolved into the modern Spring Festival traditions. Over time, the festival incorporated elements from various Chinese philosophies including Confucianism, Buddhism, and Taoism.

Traditions & Celebrations

Chinese Spring Festival is rich with traditions that have been passed down through generations. The celebration begins with a thorough cleaning of homes to sweep away bad luck, followed by decorating with red paper-cuts, couplets, and lanterns. Red envelopes (hongbao) containing money are given to children and unmarried adults for good luck.

The New Year's Eve reunion dinner is perhaps the most important part of the festival, where extended families gather for a feast featuring symbolic foods like fish (representing abundance), dumplings (wealth), and glutinous rice cakes (higher income or position). The celebrations continue with dragon and lion dances, temple fairs, and fireworks displays.

Tips for Visitors

Best Time to Visit

Arrive a few days before the New Year's Eve to experience the preparations and stay through the first week of celebrations for the most vibrant atmosphere.

Key Locations

Beijing, Shanghai, Hong Kong, and Guangzhou host some of the most spectacular celebrations. Temple fairs in Beijing and the parade in Hong Kong are particularly famous.

Traditional Foods

Try dumplings (jiaozi), spring rolls, glutinous rice cakes (nian gao), longevity noodles, and sweet rice balls (tangyuan) for an authentic experience.

Local Customs

Wear red for good luck, avoid wearing black or white (colors associated with mourning), and be prepared to receive and give red envelopes if staying with a Chinese family.
